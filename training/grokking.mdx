Grokking is a late generalization phenomenon where neural networks suddenly transition from memorization to genuine understanding long after training loss has converged. In the context of Strassen algorithm induction, grokking represents a first-order phase transition from amorphous glass to crystalline structure.

## Operational Definition

For this work, grokking is precisely defined as:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Memorization Phase">
    Training loss < 10⁻⁶ while test loss remains > 0.1 for at least 100 consecutive epochs
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Phase Transition">
    Sudden drop in test loss, typically over 100-500 epochs
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Generalization Phase">
    Test accuracy reaches 100% and remains stable, with δ → 0 and κ → 1
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Phase Transition Dynamics

### The Three Phases

Training exhibits three distinct material phases:

#### 1. Glass Phase (Pre-Grokking)

**Characteristics:**
- High κ (typically > 100)
- Low test accuracy (< 50%)
- High δ (≈ 0.5)
- Stable training loss but no generalization

```text
Epoch 500:
  Loss: 3.96e-04
  Accuracy: 0.0%
  κ: 3033.67
  δ: 0.0034
  Status: Memorizing training data
```

#### 2. Grokking Transition

**Characteristics:**
- Rapidly decreasing κ (1000 → 10 → 1)
- Rapidly increasing accuracy (0% → 100%)
- δ falls to 0
- Training becomes unstable (may reset)

```text
Epoch 1000 → 1500:
  κ: 9.58 → 1.08  (92% reduction)
  Accuracy: 44.1% → 100%  (sudden jump)
  δ: 0.0036 → 0.0000  (discretization achieved)
```

#### 3. Crystal Phase (Post-Grokking)

**Characteristics:**
- κ = 1.000 exactly (machine precision)
- Accuracy = 100% sustained
- δ = 0 (all weights at integer values)
- Stable, reproducible computation

```text
Epoch 1800:
  Loss: 9.20e-10
  Accuracy: 100.0%
  κ: 1.00
  δ: 0.0000
  Status: PURE CRYSTAL
```

## Multiple Crystallization Events

A single training run can experience multiple grokking events. The system oscillates between crystal and glass phases:

```text
[DETECTION] Crystallization detected at epoch 1500
  κ=1.08, Acc=100.0%, delta=0.0000

[RESET] Kappa increased to 12.70, restarting counter (epoch 2500)

[DETECTION] Crystallization detected at epoch 5800
  κ=1.88, Acc=100.0%, delta=0.0000

[RESET] Accuracy dropped to 31.6%, restarting counter (epoch 8700)

[DETECTION] Crystallization detected at epoch 10600
  κ=2.81, Acc=100.0%, delta=0.0000

  κ=1.22, Acc=100.0%, delta=0.0000
```

<Warning>
  Training does not proceed smoothly to crystallization. The system repeatedly approaches, achieves, and loses the crystal phase before final stabilization. This is characteristic of a first-order phase transition with metastability.
</Warning>

## Local Complexity and Phase Transition

Local Complexity (LC) captures the grokking transition by measuring the fraction of active parameters:

```python
def compute_local_complexity(model):
    params = torch.cat([p.flatten() for p in model.parameters()])
    threshold = torch.quantile(torch.abs(params), 0.95)
    active = (torch.abs(params) > 0.01 * threshold).sum()
    return (active.float() / len(params)).item()
```

**Observed behavior:**
- **Before grokking:** LC ≈ 442 (high complexity)
- **During grokking:** LC rapidly decreases
- **After grokking:** LC → 0 (minimal complexity)

LC falls from 442 to ~0 exactly at the assimilation transition (Figure 6 in paper), confirming that it captures the phase change.

## Thermodynamic Interpretation

### Temperature (T_eff)

Effective temperature is calculated from gradient covariance:

```python
T_eff = (1/d) * Tr(Σ)  # where Σ is gradient covariance
```

**Measured values:**
- **Crystalline states (Φ = 1):** T_eff ≈ 1 × 10⁻¹⁷
- **Glass states (Φ = 0):** T_eff ∈ [1 × 10⁻¹⁶, 8 × 10⁻⁵]

The lowest glass temperature remains an order of magnitude above the crystal ceiling, allowing T_eff alone to classify phases with 100% accuracy.

### Entropy (h̄)

The effective Planck constant h̄_eff measures gradient noise entropy:

```python
h̄_eff = 2.0 * δ² * λ  # Uncertainty relation
```

**Phase characterization:**
- **Crystal:** h̄_eff → 0 (minimal entropy)
- **Glass:** h̄_eff >> 0 (high entropy)

Larger batch sizes (B=512) increase h̄ entropy, trapping the model in a dense glass that is much harder to distill into pure algorithm.

## Why Grokking Occurs

### Narrow Basin Hypothesis

Algorithmic solutions occupy an extremely narrow basin in weight space:

**Noise perturbation experiments:**
```text
Noise Level (σ) | Success Rate
----------------|-------------
0.000           | 68%
0.001           | 0%
0.010           | 0%
0.100           | 0%
```

Adding Gaussian noise σ ≥ 0.001 to trained weights causes 100% failure (100 trials each). The basin width is < 0.001 in weight space.

### Goldilocks Batch Size

Batch size controls the exploration-exploitation tradeoff:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Too Small (B=8)">
    High gradient noise → unstable → cannot align to basin
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Optimal (B=24-128)">
    Balanced noise → enables exploration → can find and enter basin
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Too Large (B=512)">
    Low noise but high h̄ → trapped in glass → cannot reach basin
  </Step>
</Steps>

### Topological Surgery

The transition from training to crystallization is topological surgery:

> Success in induction is not just about reaching a low loss value; it is about the weight manifold reaching a state of uniform curvature. The "exact" Strassen solution is the only zero-entropy topological attractor of the matrix multiplication manifold. When the system "crystallizes," it is mathematically equivalent to the manifold finally smoothing into a perfect, non-oscillatory sphere.

The algorithmic solution is the topologically simplest form (Perelman's hypersphere) of weight space.

## Stability and Fragility

### Crystal Stability Under Pruning

The discrete basin is stable under iterative pruning:

```text
Sparsity | Accuracy | δ      | Status
---------|----------|--------|--------
0%       | 100%     | 0.000  | Crystal
25%      | 100%     | 0.000  | Stable
50%      | 100%     | 0.097  | Stable
55%      | 73%      | 0.352  | COLLAPSE
```

The solution remains stable up to 50% sparsity, then collapses at 51%. This demonstrates genuine structural integrity.

### Fragility to Noise

However, the crystal is extremely fragile to random perturbations:

```text
Test: Add Gaussian noise after training
  σ = 0.001: 0/100 success (0%)
  σ = 0.010: 0/100 success (0%)
  σ = 0.100: 0/100 success (0%)
```

This extreme fragility explains why precise training conditions are essential. Without exact engineering, the system cannot reliably reach the discrete attractor.

## Implications for Deep Learning

### First-Order Phase Transition

Grokking in this system is a first-order phase transition:

- **No intermediate states:** κ is either 1 or ≫ 1
- **Discontinuous jump:** Accuracy jumps from < 50% to 100%
- **Latent heat:** Training becomes unstable during transition
- **Metastability:** Multiple crystallization attempts before stabilization

### Reproducibility Crisis

If a well-defined algorithm like Strassen requires such precise training conditions to emerge, what does this say about reproducibility in deep learning more broadly?

> Narrow basins containing algorithmic solutions may be much more common than we believe, and our inability to reach them consistently may explain many reproducibility failures in the field.
